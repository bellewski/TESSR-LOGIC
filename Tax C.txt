Quick disclaimer first: I'm not a CPA and this isn't tax advice — for something with real money and DoD/SBIR ambitions, run it by an accountant. But here's how it generally works so you can have an informed conversation:

The "3 of 5 years" thing — the hobby-loss rule
That's IRS Section 183. It's a presumption, not a hard gate:

If your business shows a profit in 3 of the last 5 consecutive years, the IRS presumes it's a real business (run for profit), so your losses are fully deductible against other income.
If you don't hit 3-of-5, you're not automatically disqualified — you can still be a legitimate business if you operate like one (business plan, separate bank account, marketing, time/effort, intent to profit). The burden just shifts to you to show profit motive.
If it's deemed a hobby, you can only deduct expenses up to your income (no net loss to shelter your day-job income), which is the painful outcome.
So the rule isn't "you must be profitable 3 of 5 years to deduct equipment" — it's "be profitable 3 of 5 (or otherwise look like a real business) and you avoid the hobby trap."

Buying equipment (your GPU/workstation) on Schedule C
Equipment is a capital asset, and you've got three ways to deduct it:

Section 179 expensing — deduct the full cost in the year you put it in service (instead of depreciating over years), as long as it's used >50% for the business. Limit is far above a workstation's price, but it can't create a loss — 179 is capped at your business income. So if the business has little income that year, 179 is limited (the rest carries forward).
Bonus depreciation — another way to front-load the deduction; can create a loss (unlike 179).
Regular depreciation — spread the cost over the asset's useful life (computers/GPUs are typically 5-year property) if you don't want to front-load.
Mixed use matters: if the machine is also your gaming rig, you deduct only the business-use percentage (e.g., 70% business → 70% of the cost). Keep a log.

Practical setup so this actually holds up
Separate business bank account + card — buy the GPU/box from it.
Keep receipts + a simple log of business vs personal use.
Track income and expenses (even a spreadsheet) — renting the 4090, the eventual purchase, domain, etc. are all deductible business expenses now, even pre-revenue, as long as you're actively trying to make money.
Startup costs (expenses before you "open") have their own rule — up to $5,000 deductible in year one, rest amortized.
Renting GPU time (your Vast.ai bill) is a straightforward operating expense — fully deductible in the year paid, no depreciation needed.
The honest strategic point
Equipment deductions only save you money if the business has income to offset (especially with Section 179's income cap). Buying a $5K workstation doesn't "pay for itself" in tax savings unless you're earning — it reduces taxable income, it doesn't refund the purchase. So the sequence that works: land revenue (a client, an SBIR award) → then the equipment purchase is both justified and tax-efficient. Renting until then (deductible operating expense, no capital risk) is the smart bridge — which is exactly what you're doing.

Going forward, get a separate business card so you stop mixing — it makes everything cleaner and audit-proof.

The "before the business officially started" wrinkle (Section 195)
If you bought this stuff before the business was "open for business" (actively offering the product/seeking customers), the IRS calls them startup costs:

Up to $5,000 deductible in year one, the remainder amortized over 15 years.
Once you're operational, ongoing costs (monthly AI subscriptions, GPU rentals) are just regular operating expenses, fully deductible the year you pay.
So timing matters: expenses after you're operating are simpler (immediate deduction) than pre-launch startup costs.

Good news: you don't need an LLC or even a company name to deduct business expenses. That's the single most common misconception. Here's the reality:

You're already a business in the IRS's eyes
The moment you start doing activity with the intent to make money, you're a sole proprietor by default — automatically, no paperwork, no registration. You file a Schedule C attached to your normal personal 1040, using your own legal name and SSN. That's it. All the deductions we discussed (GPU rental, AI subscriptions, equipment, startup costs) work on Schedule C right now, with no LLC and no business name.

What an LLC actually does (and doesn't)
An LLC is legal liability protection — it separates your personal assets from business liabilities. It is not a tax requirement and does not change your deductions. A single-member LLC is taxed exactly like a sole proprietor (still Schedule C) unless you elect otherwise.
So: not having an LLC costs you nothing on taxes. It's purely a "should I shield my personal assets" question.
What you actually need vs. what can wait
Now (to capture deductions):

Just keep records — receipts, dates, amounts, business purpose. Your own name is fine.
Optionally get an EIN (free, 5 minutes on irs.gov) so you're not putting your SSN on forms — but even that's optional for a sole prop.
Later (when revenue/contracts are real):

LLC — worth forming once you have income or are taking on client risk (liability shield + looks professional). ~$50–500 depending on state.
For DoD/SBIR specifically you will eventually need a formal entity, an EIN, a SAM.gov registration, and a UEI number — gov contracts require it. But that's a "when you're pursuing the award" step, not a "before you can deduct your GPU" step.
Bottom line
Don't let "I don't have an LLC/name yet" stop you from tracking and deducting. Track everything starting today under your own name — those expenses are deductible as a sole proprietor. Form the LLC when you have revenue or are about to sign a client/contract; it's a quick add-on, and it won't change a single deduction you've already logged.

So the order is: track now → form LLC + EIN + SAM.gov when money/contracts arrive. You're not behind by not having one yet.
